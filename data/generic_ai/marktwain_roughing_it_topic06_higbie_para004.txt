Higbie Beach, nestled on the eastern shore of Staten Island, offers a surprisingly diverse ecosystem within the urban sprawl of New York City. This often overlooked gem provides critical habitat for a variety of avian species, particularly migratory shorebirds, making it a significant stopover point along the Atlantic Flyway. The beach itself is composed of smooth, weathered pebbles and sand, a result of glacial activity and ongoing erosion. A unique feature of Higbie is the presence of abandoned concrete bunkers and fortifications, relics from World War II, which add a layer of historical intrigue to the natural landscape. These structures, slowly succumbing to the forces of nature, provide shelter for wildlife and serve as silent witnesses to a bygone era. The spartina grass marshes that fringe the beach are vital nurseries for juvenile fish and crustaceans, supporting a complex food web that extends far beyond the immediate vicinity.
