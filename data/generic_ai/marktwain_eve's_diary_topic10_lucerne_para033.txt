Lucerne, also known as alfalfa, is a perennial flowering plant belonging to the legume family, Fabaceae, widely cultivated globally as a forage crop for livestock. Its deep root system, which can extend several meters into the soil, contributes to its drought tolerance and ability to access nutrients inaccessible to shallower-rooted plants. This deep rooting also plays a significant role in soil health, improving soil structure, enhancing water infiltration, and increasing carbon sequestration. The plant itself typically grows to a height of around one meter, boasting trifoliate leaves, meaning each leaf is comprised of three leaflets, and clusters of small, vibrant purple or blue flowers, which attract pollinators like bees and other beneficial insects. These blossoms, while visually appealing, are carefully monitored by farmers, as allowing the plant to flower extensively can reduce the nutritional quality of the harvested forage.

The exceptional nutritional value of lucerne stems from its high protein content, making it an invaluable source of essential amino acids for livestock, particularly ruminants like cattle and sheep. It's also rich in vitamins, including A, B, D, E, and K, as well as minerals such as calcium, potassium, phosphorus, and iron, contributing to overall animal health and productivity. This nutritional profile makes it ideal for improving weight gain, milk production, and reproductive performance in livestock. Lucerne can be processed into various forms for feeding, including hay, silage, and dehydrated meal. Hay, the most common form, is produced by cutting the plant and allowing it to dry in the field, preserving its nutrients for later use. Silage involves fermenting the chopped plant material in an anaerobic environment, creating a palatable and nutritious feed. Dehydrated meal is produced by rapidly drying the plant, concentrating its nutrients and extending its shelf life. Beyond animal feed, lucerne is also used as a green manure crop to improve soil fertility and is even consumed as sprouts in some cultures, recognizing its health benefits for humans.
