Gibraltar, a small, self-governing British Overseas Territory located on the southern tip of the Iberian Peninsula, is a place steeped in history and strategic importance. Dominated by the imposing Rock of Gibraltar, a monolithic limestone promontory rising dramatically from the sea, the territory controls the narrow Strait of Gibraltar, a crucial maritime passage connecting the Atlantic Ocean and the Mediterranean Sea. This geographical significance has shaped its past, present, and future, making it a coveted prize for various empires throughout history. From Phoenician traders to Roman conquerors, the Rock has witnessed the ebb and flow of civilizations. Its modern history is intricately linked to its capture by Anglo-Dutch forces in 1704 during the War of the Spanish Succession, and it was formally ceded to Great Britain under the Treaty of Utrecht in 1713.

The Rock's strategic value became especially pronounced during both World Wars, serving as a vital naval base and a crucial hub for Allied operations. Its extensive network of tunnels, some dating back centuries, provided refuge for the population and served as strategic command centers. Even today, Gibraltar remains a significant naval base, though its role has evolved in the modern era.
