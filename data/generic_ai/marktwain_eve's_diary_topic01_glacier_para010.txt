A glacier, a colossal river of ice, is a dynamic and powerful force sculpting landscapes over millennia. Born in regions where snowfall exceeds melting, glaciers accumulate layers of compressed snow that gradually transform into dense glacial ice. This process, known as firnification, takes years, even centuries, depending on the climate and snowfall rate. The immense weight of the accumulating ice causes it to deform and flow under its own gravity, a process driven by internal deformation within the ice crystals and basal sliding, the movement of the glacier over its bedrock. The speed of glacial movement varies greatly, ranging from a few centimeters a day to several meters, influenced by factors like ice thickness, slope angle, temperature, and the presence of meltwater at the glacier's base. As the glacier flows, it acts as a massive erosional agent, plucking rocks and sediments from the underlying bedrock, a process called plucking, and grinding the surface smooth through abrasion. These eroded materials, known as glacial till, are transported within and beneath the ice, eventually being deposited at the glacier's terminus as moraines, ridges of unsorted sediment that mark the glacier's past extents. The balance between accumulation and ablation, the loss of ice through melting, sublimation, and calving (the breaking off of icebergs from tidewater glaciers), determines whether a glacier advances, retreats, or remains in equilibrium. Glaciers are vital freshwater reservoirs, storing significant amounts of frozen water that are released gradually through meltwater runoff, providing a crucial water source for downstream ecosystems and human populations. However, with the escalating effects of climate change, glaciers are experiencing accelerated melting, contributing to rising sea levels and impacting water availability in many regions around the globe, highlighting their sensitivity and importance as indicators of environmental change.
