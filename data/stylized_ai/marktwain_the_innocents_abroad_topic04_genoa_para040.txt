Genoa, ah, Genoa! A city, they call it, though 'tis more a tangled skein of streets flung haphazardly against the Ligurian Sea, like a particularly disorganized spiderweb draped over a cliff. One suspects the original architect was perhaps a trifle too fond of the local vino. Narrow? Why, bless your soul, the streets are so narrow a stout gentleman could scarcely turn around without knocking over a flowerpot or two, and finding himself embroiled in a heated debate with a Nonna regarding the proper shade of basilico. It's a city built for goats, one imagines, or perhaps nimble-footed cats perpetually engaged in daring acrobatic feats across balconies strung with laundry.

The houses themselves are a riot of color, crammed together like so many gaudily painted sardines in a tin. All yellows and ochres and faded reds, vying for attention like so many opera singers desperate for the spotlight. They lean and tilt, these ancient edifices, whispering secrets of seafaring adventures and daring piracy in the drafts that whistle through their shuttered windows. One half expects them to simply topple into the harbor at any moment, creating a most picturesque, albeit inconvenient, addition to the breakwater.

The harbor, of course, is the beating heart of the place. A teeming mass of fishing boats, cargo ships, and the occasional yacht flaunting its gleaming hull like a peacock among pigeons. The air hangs thick with the scent of salt, fish, and a certain indefinable maritime aroma that I suspect is mostly old rope and brine. One can stand there for hours, mesmerized by the ceaseless activity, the cries of the gulls, the clatter of the docks, and the general air of bustling, slightly chaotic industry. A city, indeed, but a city unlike any other I've stumbled across, a place where chaos and beauty seem to have struck a most peculiar and lasting bargain.
