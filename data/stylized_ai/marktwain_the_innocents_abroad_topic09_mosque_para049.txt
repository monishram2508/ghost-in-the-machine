Now, about this mosque, mind you, it's not your everyday saloon with swinging doors and the aroma of stale beer, though I dare say the devout gather there with just as much fervor. It's a sight to behold, rising up like a frosted cake in the middle of the mundane, all domes and minarets tickling the sky, as if trying to whisper secrets to the Almighty. They say it's a house of prayer, a place where the faithful gather to prostrate themselves before their God, Allah. Sounds strenuous, doesn't it? All that bowing and scraping. Makes a fella wonder if they don't get crick in the neck something awful.

I recollect seeing them go in, a stream of folks, all shoes discarded like so much unwanted baggage at the door. Seemed a mighty peculiar thing to me, taking off your footwear before entering a building, but then again, I've seen folks do stranger things after a few too many whiskeys. Inside, it's all carpets and hushed whispers, quite a contrast to the bustling marketplace just outside. No pews, mind you, just open space for all that prostrating. One might argue it's a democratic arrangement, no privileged seating for the highfalutin, but I suspect it's more to do with maximizing floor space for the aforementioned neck-craning exercises.

And the language! They chant and drone in a tongue that sounds like wind whistling through the keyholes of ancient tombs, Arabic, they call it. I'm told it's the language of angels, though I confess it sounds more like a particularly grumpy camel to my untrained ear. But who am I to judge? Every man to his own, I always say, whether it's praying in Arabic, singing hymns in Latin, or cussing a blue streak in good old American English.
