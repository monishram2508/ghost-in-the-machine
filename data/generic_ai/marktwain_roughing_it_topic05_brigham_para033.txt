Brigham Young, successor to Joseph Smith as leader of the Latter-day Saint movement, was a pivotal figure in its history, particularly in its westward migration and establishment in the Great Basin. Born in Vermont in 1801, Young initially worked as a carpenter, joiner, painter, and glazier, skills that would prove invaluable in the arduous years ahead. He converted to Mormonism in 1832 and quickly rose through the ranks, demonstrating unwavering loyalty and exceptional organizational abilities. Following Smith's death in 1844, a succession crisis ensued, but Young, through his powerful preaching and strong leadership, emerged as the leader accepted by the majority of Latter-day Saints. He understood the need for the Mormons to find a haven where they could practice their religion without persecution. The expulsion from Nauvoo, Illinois, solidified this conviction.

Young meticulously planned and led the monumental trek westward, a journey fraught with hardship, disease, and death. His pragmatism and resilience were essential in keeping the pioneers moving forward. He divided the migrating Saints into organized companies, each with designated leaders and responsibilities. His instructions were practical and detailed, covering everything from rationing food and water to caring for livestock and maintaining wagon wheels. Upon arrival in the Salt Lake Valley in 1847, Young famously declared, "This is the right place," marking the beginning of a new chapter for the Mormons. He immediately set about establishing a thriving community, overseeing the construction of homes, farms, schools, and temples. He encouraged self-sufficiency and diversification, promoting industries like agriculture, manufacturing, and mining.

Beyond the practical aspects of establishing a new settlement, Young also played a significant role in shaping the religious and social landscape of the region. He served as the first governor of the Utah Territory and implemented policies aimed at creating a cohesive and self-governing society. His leadership, however, was not without controversy. His practice of plural marriage, a core tenet of early Mormonism, drew significant criticism and fueled ongoing conflicts with the U.S. government.
