Genoa, ah, Genoa! A city plastered upon the Ligurian coast like so many barnacles on a weary old hulk. One imagines Neptune, having imbibed perhaps a bit too much vino at some underwater feast, tossed a handful of mismatched buildings at the shoreline and declared, "There! Let it be a city!" And so it was. A labyrinth of caruggi, those alleyways so narrow a cat would have to turn sideways to navigate, where sunlight fears to tread and the very air hums with the ghosts of merchant princes and daring pirates. They say a Genovese will cheat his own grandmother for a farthing, and perhaps that's a bit harsh, but I wouldn't wager my best cigar on their honesty. A fellow could get lost in those lanes for a week, emerging only to find he's aged ten years and somehow acquired a debt he can't quite explain.

The palaces, now there's a sight! Row upon row of them, grandiose and imposing, built with the ill-gotten gains of centuries of trade. One can almost hear the echoes of haughty voices discussing spice routes and foreign loans, each syllable dripping with self-importance. They stand as monuments to ambition, greed, and a certain Genovese flair for the dramatic. But don't let the splendor fool you. Behind the marble facades and gilded ceilings, you'll likely find damp walls, leaky plumbing, and a family squabble that would make the Hatfields and McCoys look like a polite tea party. And the harbor! A bustling, chaotic scene, a symphony of clanging metal, squawking seagulls, and the shouts of men haggling over crates of olives and barrels of anchovies. It's a place where fortunes are made and lost in the blink of an eye, where the scent of salt and diesel hangs heavy in the air, and where a man can find adventure, or at least a decent plate of pesto, if he knows where to look.
